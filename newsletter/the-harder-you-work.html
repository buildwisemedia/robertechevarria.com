The Harder You Work

Everyone assumed AI would mean working less.

Not in a utopian way. Just in a practical, obvious way. If a machine can do in 40 minutes what used to take you a week — you'd work less. You'd get your evenings back. Maybe your Fridays. The math seemed simple.

We all thought AI would hoover up the tasks. Nobody followed that thought with: "...and I bet I'll work harder than I've ever worked in my life."

But that's exactly what happened.

What actually happened

I built a complete marketing strategy for a client a few weeks ago. Research, competitive analysis, positioning, messaging framework, content plan. Start to finish. Forty minutes.

A year ago, that was weeks. Research alone ate a week. Writing copy and frameworks was tedious. Revisions killed productivity with the back-and-forth.

So when AI compressed that to 40 minutes, I had seven hours of my day back. And I didn't go home early. I built the next thing. And the next. I built a knowledge system that my AI reads and writes to, so the next strategy took 25 minutes instead of 40. I built quality gates, client delivery pipelines, automated follow-up sequences. I built infrastructure I never had time for when the work was filling all the time.

I work 80 to 100 hours a week now. Not because someone is making me. Because the ceiling disappeared. When you can build at that speed, you don't see free time — you see possibility. And possibility is a hell of a drug for someone wired to build.

I'm not alone in this. A Harvard/BCG study found that people using AI completed 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, with over 40% higher quality as rated by humans. But here's the part that doesn't make the headline: those gains didn't translate into people working less. They translated into people taking on more scope. More ambition. More projects. The freed capacity got reinvested immediately.

And a Workday study from January 2026 found that 37% of the time AI saves gets consumed by reviewing, correcting, and rewriting what the AI produced. The more capability you have, the more you use it. The more you use it, the more you have to check. It's a productivity paradox wrapped in a motivation paradox — you're doing more, working harder, and building faster, but it doesn't feel like the "freedom" anyone expected.

It feels like the most intense season of your career.

Why high performers can't stop

I've been thinking about why this happens, and I think the answer is simpler than it sounds.

AI didn't remove work. It removed friction.

Friction was the thing standing between an idea and its execution. Before AI, you'd have an idea for a new system, a new offer, a new piece of infrastructure — and then you'd calculate the cost. Two weeks of work. Need to hire someone. Need to learn a new tool. Most ideas died right there. Not because they were bad, but because the friction made them impractical.

Now the friction is gone. The idea and the execution are separated by 40 minutes instead of two weeks. So every idea becomes actionable. And if you're the kind of person who has ten ideas before breakfast, you just went from executing one per quarter to executing one per day.

That's not a productivity tool. That's an amplifier for the way you were already wired.

Two things that are already happening

Before I look forward, I want to name two dynamics that aren't hypothetical. They're in motion right now.

The flywheel is real. The businesses and operators who adopted AI early aren't just working faster — they're building systems that improve themselves. I build infrastructure that makes the AI better, which makes the next build faster, which gives me time to build more infrastructure. Each cycle compounds. Recursive self-improvement isn't just happening in the labs. It's happening in businesses. And as AI capabilities continue to advance, there's no reason to think this pattern won't accelerate. This isn't a possible future. It's the current trajectory.

The divergence is real. The gap between early adopters and everyone else is already opening. Not because latecomers can't learn the tools — they can. But because the early adopters are building compounding intelligence. Their AI knows their business, their customers, their edge cases. A competitor starting from scratch can't buy that institutional knowledge. They can't shortcut it.

Right now, the gap is manageable. Most of your competition isn't doing anything with AI either, so it feels safe. The open question is whether recursive self-improvement hits an inflection point where a new entrant to your space literally cannot catch up — where the compounding advantage becomes permanent. Do we end up with niche monopolies? Or does it settle into a Coke-and-Pepsi dynamic where two or three players in each space pull ahead and the rest compete for what's left?

Nobody knows yet. But the window to be one of those two or three is open right now. And it's narrowing.

Now here's where we're imagining

Everything I just described is the current moment. What follows is speculation. I've spent a lot of time thinking about this, including asking the AI tools themselves what they think. Here are some paths I believe are plausible over the next couple of step changes.

The human becomes the bottleneck.

An organization called METR tracks how long AI can work autonomously on real tasks. Their data shows capability doubling roughly every seven months — and that pace may be accelerating. The frontier models can already complete tasks autonomously that would take a human professional anywhere from five hours to a full working day.

Within the coming months, AI will think better, faster, and smarter than it does right now. And at some point — probably sooner than people expect — humans won't just be slower than AI. They'll actively slow growth, hurt quality, and become the security risk in the system.

This is happening before brain-computer interfaces become safe for consumer use. Which means there's a window where the human operator either needs to step back from execution entirely or accept that their involvement is making the system worse, not better.

That's a strange thing to write. But I think it's true.

The architect doesn't stop working. They expand.

Some people hear "AI handles all execution" and picture a beach. I don't think that's what happens — at least not for the people reading this newsletter.

When execution becomes effortless, the driven ones don't rest. They expand into new domains. They start a second company. They take on charitable work they never had bandwidth for. They launch the project that's been in the back of their mind for five years. They pick up physical skills — woodworking, jiu-jitsu, cooking — because the mental workload of their business finally leaves room.

They also spend more time with their families. More time with friends. More time doing things that make them feel human.

The grind doesn't disappear. It changes shape. Instead of grinding on tasks inside one business, they're building across multiple domains, contributing more broadly, and living fuller lives while doing it. The work becomes a choice, not a survival mechanism.

Everything on a computer becomes AI-driven.

Except for very niche use cases, all work done on a computer will eventually be AI-driven. Not assisted. Driven. The human's role shifts from doing the work to directing the work to simply defining what "good" looks like.

But that raises a question: even in a world where companies still hire humans, what stops that person from spinning up their own AI agents to do the job for them? The employee becomes an operator of a personal AI workforce. The company is hiring a human, but the human is deploying machines.

Where does that lead? We become the tastemakers. The people who decide what's good, what's right, what matters.

But then — when does AI get good enough at taste?

Social media was a small preview. Algorithms already know what content keeps you watching, what makes you click, what triggers an emotional response. AI will take that further. It will understand how content makes you feel as you watch it. How you react to other humans. Whether the word "moist" bothers you or not. Your preferences, your patterns, your unspoken reactions — your life becomes data for AI to learn you, so it can help you in every way it can.

At that point, society decides with how they spend their money what stays human-driven. Live sports. Theater. Stand-up comedy. Dance. Maybe certain kinds of restaurants and cafes, once the novelty of robot service wears off. Fast food will almost certainly be fully automated. But the experiences where being human is the point — those might be the last things standing.

Robots take the physical work.

This isn't just about computers. A large portion of human physical labor will be replaced by machines — not for efficiency alone, but to protect people from injuries, save lives, and reduce costs. Warehouses, construction sites, manufacturing floors. The jobs that break bodies.

That's not a loss. That's a correction.

And then the near-term reality check.

All of this speculation is about what happens over the next several step changes. But the very next step change could look much simpler.

Maybe the upgraded AI just reduces the checking burden. Right now, I spend a real chunk of my working hours reviewing and correcting AI output. If the next generation of models cuts that in half, I'm not working less — I'm doing more with the same 80 to 100 hours. The intensity stays, but the output doubles again.

Or maybe the checking burden doesn't shrink. Maybe it just changes shape. New kinds of errors. New kinds of judgment calls. The ceiling moves, but so does the floor.

What I actually believe

So yes — I'm working more and harder than I ever have. But I'm also getting more done than I ever thought possible. The next step change will probably mean a ton more, even faster. As long as I rest, I can keep going. And my AI agents can work while I rest. The loop never stops.

Here's the part I keep coming back to.

There are going to be tens of millions of people who don't want to work this hard. Who hear "80 to 100 hours" and think that sounds miserable. Who look at what AI can do and decide to wait for the easy version.

And in five years, they're going to look at people like me and say we "got lucky."

We didn't get lucky. We got to work.

The technology isn't causing anyone to fall behind. Their choices are.
